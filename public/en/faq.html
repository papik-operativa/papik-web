An honest guide to the questions clients ask us most often

Group 1 · About Passivhaus

1. What exactly is a Passivhaus home?

A Passivhaus home is a dwelling designed under an international standard that limits the energy demand for heating and cooling to a maximum of 15 kWh/m²·year, and total primary energy use to 120 kWh/m²·year. It is not a commercial brand: it is a technical protocol developed by the Passivhaus Institut in Darmstadt (Germany), verified through PHPP calculation and tested on site with a blower door test that confirms airtightness below 0.6 air changes per hour at 50 Pa.

In practice, you walk into a Passivhaus and notice three things: temperature is uniform between rooms (no cold corners), there are no draughts, and the air is fresh and dry thanks to mechanical ventilation with heat recovery. Energy bills drop between 75 and 90 per cent compared to a conventional home built to minimum Spanish CTE requirements. It is not just thicker insulation: it is an integrated system of envelope, thermal bridges, airtightness and ventilation.

If you want to dig into the technical fundamentals, read the five Passivhaus principles.

2. How much do you save in a Passivhaus home?

Savings depend on climate, the baseline house and energy prices. As a useful reference for Catalonia, a 200 m² Passivhaus single-family home consumes between 2,000 and 3,500 kWh/year for climate control, against 12,000 to 18,000 kWh in a conventional house built fifteen years ago. Translated into euros, that is an annual bill of 250 to 450 euros against the usual 1,800 to 3,000 euros.

Across the home's full service life (50 years, conservative), accumulated savings range from 90,000 to 150,000 euros, without counting energy price escalation. This is the economic reason a Passivhaus, with a construction premium of 5 to 12 per cent, pays back between year 8 and year 14. From there onward, it is net benefit to the owner.

To this you should add savings on boiler maintenance (there are none), on future improvement works (the envelope is already done) and the practical removal of energy poverty risk in the face of tariff hikes.

3. Is a Passivhaus more expensive?

Yes, in direct construction costs: between 5 and 12 per cent more than an equivalent conventional house, depending on the brief. The difference concentrates in continuous insulation, high-performance windows and doors, the mechanical ventilation system with heat recovery and rigorous execution control (airtightness tests, thermal bridge supervision).

That said, "more expensive" is the wrong way to frame it. The right question is: more expensive compared to what, and over how long? If you compare a Passivhaus to a CTE-minimum home over twenty years (with bills, maintenance and possible energy retrofits included), the Passivhaus comes out cheaper. Compared over fifty years, the gap widens substantially.

There is another relevant nuance: green mortgages offer reduced rates for homes with A rating, and NGEU grants can cover part of the premium in retrofit projects. For a detailed breakdown of what a Passivhaus actually costs in Catalonia in 2026, see our passive house budget guide.

4. Does Passivhaus work in Mediterranean climates?

It works, but it must be designed differently. The Passivhaus standard was born in Germany, and the first generation of homes from northern Europe prioritised solar gain and heat conservation in winter. In Mediterranean climate the dominant problem is the opposite: solar protection in summer, thermal mass and night ventilation.

A well-designed Mediterranean Passivhaus uses glass with a lower solar factor on south and west facades, exterior moveable solar protection (not just interior blinds, which act too late), appropriate thermal masses and, often, low-energy active cooling integrated into the ventilation system. Airtightness remains critical, and so does insulation, but the PHPP calculation accounts for local solar radiation and adjusts parameters.

We have certified Passivhaus dwellings in Vallès, Maresme and Garrotxa (counties in Catalonia, Spain), and all of them get through summers with stable thermal comfort, free from the overheating pathologies that affect some poorly dimensioned projects. If you want to go deeper, we have published a specific article on Passivhaus in Mediterranean climate.

Group 2 · About PAPIK and Eskimohaus®

5. What is PAPIK's Eskimohaus® system?

Eskimohaus® is our proprietary construction system, developed over more than a decade of Passivhaus projects in Catalonia. It is not a closed modular product nor an assembly kit: it is a project-and-build methodology that combines a mixed structure of glulam timber and CLT, a continuous envelope using natural insulation (wood fibre, cellulose) and an execution protocol with intermediate checks on airtightness and thermal continuity.

The advantage for the client is that every project starts from a base that is already technically resolved. We do not reinvent the corner solution, the window detail or the roof-to-wall connection on every job. This translates into fewer site surprises, more predictable timelines and a repeatable level of quality. The architectural signature, however, is always singular: aesthetics and layout are designed from scratch for each client.

Eskimohaus® is registered as a trademark and is not licensed to third parties. It is built only with PAPIK Group as contractor, or with the firms that form part of our laminated timber consortium.

6. How does PAPIK differ from other Passivhaus builders?

Three elements we consider relevant. First, vertical control: PAPIK designs, executes, certifies and hands over. We do not subcontract Passivhaus direction to external practices, which avoids the information asymmetry between designer and builder that often inflates costs midway through the works.

Second, the patrimonial focus. We work with families who want to pass the home on to the next generation, not just to occupy it themselves. This changes decisions on materials (durability, maintenance, A1 fire classes where appropriate), on construction details (future accessibility) and on documentation (delivery of a complete technical archive, not just the building log).

Third, cost discipline. Our quotes include explicit contingency and no hidden options. What you see on page one of the quote is what you pay at the end, except for changes you request. We have held this policy since our first project, and it is the reason 70 per cent of our pipeline comes from direct referrals by previous clients.

Group 3 · About the construction process

7. How long does it take to build a Passivhaus home?

Between 14 and 24 months from preliminary design commission to handover, depending on size and complexity. The typical breakdown for a 200 to 300 m² single-family home is: 4 to 6 months of design and approval, 3 to 5 months of planning permission processing (variable by municipality), 8 to 12 months of construction, and 1 month of final tests and Passivhaus certification.

These timelines assume a clean plot, with obtainable permits and no complications such as demolition files or change-of-use procedures. A Passivhaus on a complicated plot (steep slope, land cessions, access roads) can add 3 to 6 months to the pre-construction phase. The Eskimohaus® construction phase, however, is fairly stable: the timber structure is erected in 4 to 6 weeks and the envelope is closed in another 8 to 10 weeks.

If you need a shorter timeline (for a sale closing, for example, or a family calendar), say so at the first meeting. There are project decisions (parallelising procedures, freezing changes earlier, prefabrication) that can trim 2 to 4 months off the total.

8. Do I need a specific kind of plot to build Passivhaus?

No, but some plots are better than others for the final cost. The best Passivhaus plot has south or southeast orientation free of winter shadows, dimensions that allow separation from party walls (to avoid complex thermal bridges) and moderate slope (under 15 per cent saves a lot in earthworks).

That said, we have built Passivhaus on urban plots 6 metres wide between party walls, on corners with two north-facing facades, on 30 per cent slopes and on plots with water easements. Everything is solvable. What changes is the cost: a pure north orientation can add between 3 and 7 per cent to the budget (more insulation, more technical glazing, possible pre-conditioned ventilation). A steep slope adds 5 to 15 per cent in structural cost.

The only non-negotiable condition is municipal planning compliance. Before you buy a plot with Passivhaus in mind, run it past us with the cadastral plan and the current local plan: in one hour we can tell you whether your project will fit.

9. Can I build Passivhaus on a sloped plot?

Yes. Slope is not a technical problem for Passivhaus: it is a design and cost problem solved with two strategies. The first, stepped houses that follow the level change, distributing the brief across connected platforms. This is often the best aesthetic solution and the most economical when the slope is regular.

The second, houses on a ventilated crawl-space slab with a retaining wall. Useful when the slope is irregular or when you want to keep the natural terrain visible. Structural cost is higher, but it allows free floor plans and large cantilevered terraces.

The Passivhaus envelope behaves equally well in either solution, provided three details are carefully resolved: insulation continuity at the wall-to-slab joint (this is where most projects fail the blower door), perimeter drainage and the treatment of vegetation at the lower level (rising damp). We have documented these details in projects in Vallès Oriental and Berguedà (counties in Catalonia, Spain), where the topography is never flat.

Group 4 · About retrofit

10. Is it worth retrofitting an old house to Passivhaus?

Sometimes yes, sometimes no. The right question is whether it is worth doing an EnerPHit (the Passivhaus standard for retrofit), not whether to retrofit at all. EnerPHit is a relaxed version of Passivhaus that allows 25 kWh/m²·year of heating demand (instead of 15) precisely because it starts from existing geometry that limits what can be done.

EnerPHit makes sense when: the masia, village house or dwelling has heritage or emotional value worth preserving; the existing structure is in good condition (no serious pathology); and the envelope can be intervened from the exterior without losing habitable area. In those cases, energy savings are comparable to a new build and the investment pays back in 12 to 18 years.

It does not make sense when: the structure has pathology requiring expensive reinforcement; the house geometry makes a continuous envelope impossible; or the foreseeable cost exceeds an equivalent new build by more than 30 per cent. In that case, demolition and new construction is more honest and often better.

For a quantitative analysis with real numbers, read our comparative article: replace vs. retrofit.

11. Is Passivhaus compatible with NGEU grants?

Yes, and in fact Passivhaus and EnerPHit are among the highest-scoring standards in the programmes in force until 31 December 2026. Next Generation EU grants for residential retrofit in Catalonia are channelled through programmes 3, 4 and 5, managed by the Agència de l'Habitatge de Catalunya.

In broad terms: programme 3 funds building retrofits (up to 80 per cent of cost with energy savings above 60 per cent), programme 4 funds individual dwelling interventions (up to 14,000 euros), and programme 5 funds the building log and the design fees. A well-documented EnerPHit usually accesses the maximum of programme 3 because it certifies reductions of 70 to 90 per cent.

There is important fine print: justification deadlines, grant compatibilities, tax requirements and limitations for rented properties. This answer is general guidance, not tax or legal advice. For your specific situation, consult a specialised adviser or request a meeting with our grants team. All up-to-date information is in our NGEU guide for Catalonia.

Group 5 · About certification

12. How is a Passivhaus home certified?

Passivhaus certification is an independent verification process that runs in three phases. First, the PHPP calculation, the official Passivhaus Institut spreadsheet where all project data is entered (geometry, materials, thermal bridges, ventilation, shading, local climate) to obtain the energy demand. The designer produces this calculation; an accredited certifier reviews it.

Second, on-site control, where an independent certifier (not the PAPIK architect) supervises the execution of critical details. It includes the airtightness test (blower door) at two moments: an interim test at envelope close-up (when whatever fails can still be repaired) and a final test with the house complete. The result must be below 0.6 air changes per hour at 50 Pa for Passivhaus or 1.0 for EnerPHit.

Third, documentary submission to the Passivhaus Institut or an official certifying body (Passive House Database, PEP, ZEPHIR), which issues the numbered certificate. The process has an additional cost on top of design fees (between 4,000 and 8,000 euros depending on size) which we recommend assuming: a "de facto Passivhaus" without certificate loses asset value and does not access green mortgages.

For an in-depth explanation of the EnerPHit process, see our complete EnerPHit pillar.

Group 6 · About B2B and family offices

13. Does PAPIK work with family offices?

Yes. We have a specific service line, Patrimonios, designed for family offices, wealth managers and private clients operating from a portfolio logic. The difference from a standard residential commission is not the product (which remains a high-end Passivhaus build) but the process: communication with a designated wealth manager, financial documentation adapted to family accounting standards, quarterly reporting calendars and reinforced confidentiality agreements.

We work with two types of operation. The first, primary or secondary residence for the principal family, where the client is final and the decision is made by the head of the family with family-office advice. The second, patrimonial operations: dwellings for future generations, second homes destined for high-end rental, or controlled developments with a closed number of units.

We do not pay commissions and we do not issue binding offers by phone. The entry door is always a private conversation with one of the founding partners, under a confidentiality agreement. If you represent a family office or a wealth advisory, write to us directly and we will reply with a first-meeting proposal.

Group 7 · Comparisons and durability

14. Modular or Passivhaus?

They are not mutually exclusive, but they answer to different priorities. Industrialised modular construction prioritises short timelines (4 to 8 months on site), a fixed catalogue price and future flexibility (the house can be extended by adding modules). Quality ceiling is high in good manufacturers, but it rarely reaches the detailing and singularity of a bespoke build.

Passivhaus prioritises certified energy performance, durability (50 to 100 years of service life with normal maintenance) and architectural signature. It is a quality system, not a speed system. A modular Passivhaus is possible (we have built them) but it requires a manufacturer specialised in Passivhaus detailing, not just any modular vendor.

For the typical PAPIK client (a family designing a home for one or two generations, with their own plot and a quality demand), bespoke Passivhaus is almost always the right option. For a client who wants a quick, no-fuss second residence with a fixed budget, modular may make sense. We have developed this topic in a specific article: modular vs. Passivhaus.

15. Is a Passivhaus home for life?

Yes, and this is one of its most robust economic arguments. A well-built Passivhaus has a service life of 50 to 100 years with simple maintenance. The envelope (insulation, windows, airtightness) is designed to last the building's whole life. The mechanical ventilation system has a service life of 15 to 25 years depending on manufacturer, with filter changes every 6 months and an annual review.

What needs maintaining? Heat recovery filters (low annual cost), cleaning of exterior air intakes (once a year), inspection of window seals (every 5 to 10 years, depending on exposure) and exterior paint or treatment if the facade requires it (every 10 to 15 years depending on material). No boilers, no gas inspections, no tanks.

The generational argument matters for families thinking about wealth on a long horizon. A Passivhaus delivered in 2026 to your son or daughter in 2056 will still be a high energy-rated home, with no need for structural retrofit. This is not the case for most homes built to minimum CTE standards, which after 30 years will require significant energy interventions to stay current.
