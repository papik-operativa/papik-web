Sustainable materials: timber, cork, cellulose insulation and other real options

Which sustainable materials are actually used in passive houses in Catalonia. CLT timber, cork, cellulose insulation, Accoya. Fourteen years of field experience by PAPIK.

What defines a sustainable material

Before the specific materials, the assessment framework must be established. A material can be considered sustainable when it meets at least four criteria: renewable or recycled origin, low manufacturing impact with reduced embodied energy, durability compatible with the service life of the building (fifty to one hundred years), and recyclability or biodegradability at the end of its service life.

The materials that satisfy these four criteria are few and well known in the sector. Our selection, the result of fourteen years of experience building high energy efficiency homes, focuses on four families that offer the best balance across each of the four criteria.

Timber: structure and cladding

Timber is the most used sustainable material in PAPIK's Eskimohaus® system. We work with three main typologies that are chosen according to the project and the structural requirements.

Cross-laminated timber (CLT)

Laminated timber panels with layers alternated in perpendicular directions. Structurally resistant, lightweight, prefabricable. Its natural application is the complete structure of the house (walls, floors, roofs), originating from European forests with FSC or PEFC certification.

The main advantage is that it offers structural capacity equivalent to concrete with a negative CO₂ footprint, since the timber sequesters carbon during the tree's growth and keeps it captured while the building exists. The limitation is the higher initial cost compared to conventional lightweight framing, which is why it is not the first choice for every project.

Lightweight timber framing

Structure based on timber studs and crosspieces of sawn wood, with insulation inside the cavities. It is the base construction system of most Eskimohaus® houses for two combined reasons: economy of materials and labour, and compatibility with industrial prefabrication. The limitation compared to CLT is the lower thermal inertia, which can be compensated for with phase change materials (PCM) or with other building elements.

Accoya timber

Pine timber acetylated through a chemical process that makes it virtually rot-resistant, with a service life of more than fifty years outdoors without additional treatment. Its natural application is exterior cladding, shutters and elements exposed to the weather.

The advantage is the extraordinary durability with minimal maintenance, which in some projects significantly extends the service life of the cladding. The higher cost compared to conventional alternatives limits its application to cases where durability justifies the investment.

Cork

Natural cork comes from the bark of the cork oak (Quercus suber) and is harvested every nine years without harming the tree. It is a structurally lightweight, hydrophobic, biodegradable material with outstanding thermal and acoustic performance. At PAPIK we use it as thermal insulation in agglomerated cork boards (density 100 to 150 kg/m³, thermal conductivity around 0.040 W/mK), as exterior cladding on facades (hydrophobic, requires no additional treatment), and as acoustic insulation in floors and partition walls.

The sustainability of cork is documented with an unusual argument in the sector: the regeneration cycle is the opposite of that of most industrial materials. Harvesting cork actively contributes to the health of the Mediterranean cork forest, which makes its use a regenerative practice, not a neutral one.

The limitation is cost. Premium cork, especially for exterior facades, has a higher price than conventional SATE, which is why its application is considered in projects where aesthetics and exterior durability are explicit priorities.

Blown cellulose insulation

Blown cellulose insulation is an insulation material made from recycled paper (typically newspapers) treated with mineral salts for fire and insect resistance. It is installed by blowing it into the cavities of the structure with specific machinery.

The advantages are notable. The origin is recycled: approximately one cubic metre of cellulose insulation is equivalent to about thirty or forty kilos of recycled paper. It is hygroscopic, which makes it a natural regulator of indoor humidity, since it absorbs and releases moisture according to ambient conditions. Its embodied energy is significantly lower than that of other insulation materials: around 0.2 to 0.3 kWh/kg, compared to 4 to 6 kWh/kg for mineral wool. And its performance is equivalent to rock wool, with thermal conductivity between 0.038 and 0.040 W/mK.

At PAPIK we use it as the main insulation in many Eskimohaus® system projects. It was part of the Passive House in La Floresta project in Sant Cugat, our first project that combined CLT with blown cellulose insulation. The limitation is that it requires professional installation with specific machinery, which is why it is not a material that a builder can apply without proper training and equipment.

Other sustainable materials and elements

Beyond the three pillars (timber, cork and cellulose insulation), we incorporate other materials ad hoc according to the needs of each project. High-density mineral wool solves some specific applications where its particular properties (fire resistance, density) justify the use. Hygrovariable membranes regulate water vapour in the envelope (see Passivhaus Innovations 2026). Mineral paints without volatile organic compounds (VOC) ensure indoor air quality. Natural wood floors (oak, ash, chestnut, ipe depending on application) and parquet or microcement of mineral origin cover the different areas of the home according to functional requirements.

Everything that enters an Eskimohaus® site meets at least the first criterion (renewable or recycled origin) and preferably all four.

Lessons from fourteen years of experience

Some practical conclusions from our field experience are valuable for any owner who is considering a sustainable project.

The first is that not all materials labelled as "sustainable" on the market really are. Some commercial datasheets highlight a specific attribute (renewable origin, for example) and hide the other three (embodied energy, durability, end of service life). Reading the EPD (Environmental Product Declaration), if available, is the reliable way to verify the manufacturer's claims.

The second is that the appropriate material depends on the local climate. Materials that work perfectly in northern Europe may behave differently in the Mediterranean climate with hot, humid summers. Cellulose insulation, cork and native timber are options especially well adapted to our climatic context, which is why they carry more weight in our selection.

The third is that cost is not linear with sustainability. CLT timber has a cost similar to concrete of equivalent structure. Cellulose insulation has a cost similar to mineral wool. Premium cork is more expensive than conventional SATE. Each decision has its own cost-benefit balance, and not all projects nor all budgets justify the most sustainable option in every line item.

The fourth is that sustainability is built in the detail. The best insulation in the world does not compensate for poor execution of the vapour barrier. The best cladding does not compensate for a structural thermal bridge. Sustainability is the sum of many small decisions consistent with each other, not a big decision that can be delegated to the material.

Frequently asked questions

What is the most sustainable material for the structure?

CLT timber with FSC or PEFC certification. It has positive carbon capture and good durability. For many projects it is economically viable, especially when total costs are compared over the life cycle and not just the initial investment.

Is cork more expensive than conventional SATE?

Yes. The cost difference is assessed case by case. It makes sense when aesthetics and exterior durability are explicit priorities of the project, or when the municipal ordinance favours natural materials.

What is an EPD?

Environmental Product Declaration. A standardised technical document according to the ISO 14025 standard that certifies the environmental impact of a material throughout its life cycle. When a manufacturer does not provide one, it is good reason to be suspicious of their sustainable advertising.

Which materials do you use for exterior facades?

Common combinations are SATE, ventilated facade with timber (Accoya or equivalent), natural cork, and fibre cement. Each project selects according to specific context, ordinance and budget.
