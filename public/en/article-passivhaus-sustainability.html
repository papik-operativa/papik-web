Passivhaus and sustainability: technical framework, LCA and verifiable decisions

Sustainability as a technical framework, not a label

A home is not sustainable because it has timber cladding, photovoltaic panels or a marketing brochure that says so. It is sustainable when it meets three conditions at once: it reduces its environmental impact across every phase of the lifecycle, it does so with a normalised methodology, and the improvement is quantifiable against a reference scenario.

The Passivhaus standard tackles the use phase (energy operation) at a level no European regulation matches. But to talk about sustainability with rigour you also need to look at the embodied carbon of materials, the traceability of sources (FSC, PEFC), structural durability over fifty or a hundred years, and end-of-life behaviour. This is where standard EN 15978 (Sustainability of construction works · Assessment of environmental performance of buildings · Calculation method) stops being an acronym and becomes a decision tool.

EN 15978 and Lifecycle Assessment applied to a home

Lifecycle Assessment (LCA) calculates a building's environmental impact across five major phases. Phases A1 to A3 cover the product: raw material extraction, transport and manufacturing. Phases A4 and A5 cover construction: transport to site and the construction process itself. Phases B1 to B7 cover use: operation, maintenance, repair, replacement, energy and water. Phases C1 to C4 cover end of life: demolition, transport, treatment and disposal. Phase D captures benefits beyond the system boundary, such as reuse and recycling.

The most widely used indicator is Global Warming Potential (GWP), expressed in kgCO₂eq per useful square metre. A conventional home built to current technical code typically lands between 800 and 1,200 kgCO₂eq/m² across fifty years of operation. A well-designed Passivhaus with low-impact materials can cut that figure by 40% to 60%, and a build using CLT structure with biobased insulation can drop below 300 kgCO₂eq/m² net thanks to stored biogenic carbon.

When someone calls a building a "sustainable home" without referencing EN 15978, LCA, or Environmental Product Declaration (EPD), the conversation is commercial, not technical.

Embodied carbon: the forgotten phase

Sustainable architecture has historically focused on cutting operational consumption (phase B6). That made sense while European homes consumed hundreds of kilowatt hours per square metre per year. With the arrival of the Passivhaus standard, operational consumption has been compressed close to physical limits, and the most relevant phase of the lifecycle is now embodied carbon.

In a current Passivhaus home, embodied carbon (phases A1 to A5) can account for 50% to 70% of the total impact across fifty years. That shift means material decisions, from structure to finishes, are no longer an aesthetic detail but the main lever of environmental performance.

Structure is the heaviest decision. A reinforced concrete frame contributes between 250 and 400 kgCO₂eq/m² of built area. A hybrid timber structure using glulam or CLT, well designed, can land between 50 and 100 kgCO₂eq/m² net once stored biogenic carbon is counted. Continuous insulation (SATE, External Thermal Insulation Composite System) also offers leverage: insulating with blown cellulose or sprayed cork stores carbon, while mineral wool or EPS contribute net emissions.

Materials, traceability and formal certifications

A material decision is not sustainable just because the material is "natural". It is sustainable when it comes with a verified EPD, supply chain traceability and a favourable balance of embodied energy once transport is considered. Mineral wool imported from the other end of Europe can have a worse footprint than a recycled insulation of local origin, even if it sounds less green.

For timber, the reference certifications are FSC (Forest Stewardship Council) and PEFC (Programme for the Endorsement of Forest Certification). Both certify that the wood comes from forests managed under verifiable environmental and social criteria. For biobased insulation, serious manufacturers publish EPDs under ISO 14025 with GWP and other impact indicators. For overall project rating, schemes such as Passivhaus PHI, EnerPHit (for retrofit of existing homes), LEED and BREEAM add complementary layers of verification.

The absence of these certifications does not prove poor practice, but their presence is the only operational way to tell a sustainable project apart from one that simply markets itself as such.

Operation: why Passivhaus remains decisive

Even though embodied carbon has gained visibility, operation remains the most robust long-term lever, especially in climates with long summers and electricity mixes still leaning on fossil sources.

A home that meets the technical code in climate zone C2 allows wall U-values up to 0.75 W/m²K and may consume between 40 and 60 kWh/m²·a for heating and cooling. A Passivhaus operates in another order of magnitude: walls with U-values between 0.10 and 0.15 W/m²K, maximum air permeability of 0.6 air changes per hour at 50 Pa, peak heating and cooling demand below 15 kWh/m²·a.

This jump is not academic. It translates into energy bills 75% to 90% lower, into stable indoor temperatures without high mechanical capacity, and into resilience against energy price volatility that no other construction strategy can match with the same guarantee. Compounded over fifty years, the operational saving of a Passivhaus over a conventional home can exceed 170 tonnes of CO₂ avoided for an average dwelling.

If you want the numerical detail behind this balance, the article The ecological footprint of building and living in your home breaks it down phase by phase.

Mediterranean bioclimatic design and hundred-year durability

Passivhaus was born in Central Europe and its original challenge was retaining heat. In the Mediterranean the dominant problem is the long, hot summer, which is why passive design has to adapt. Window orientation, overhangs calculated for the exact site latitude, mobile solar shading, night-time cross ventilation and thermal mass in slabs and solid walls are the tools that secure summer comfort without intensive mechanical cooling.

Structural durability is a frequently overlooked component of sustainability. A home designed to last a hundred years spreads its embodied carbon over twice the time of a building meant to last fifty. Detail decisions (moisture management, thermal bridge control, accessibility of services for maintenance, quality of fixings and joints) therefore carry environmental weight equal to or greater than the headline material itself.

This integrated approach, combining low-carbon materials, climate-adapted passive design and technical durability, defines the Eskimohaus® method we apply to PAPIK residential projects, both in new construction (construction) and in interventions on existing buildings (retrofit).

ESG: sustainability as a patrimonial asset

For private wealth owners, family offices and investment funds, sustainability has stopped being an ethical preference and become a financial factor. The ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance) framework and the European taxonomy for sustainable finance classify real estate assets according to verifiable environmental criteria, and access to preferential financing depends increasingly on that classification.

A certified Passivhaus home, with documented LCA under EN 15978 and EPDs for the principal materials, fits naturally within the European taxonomy heading of "substantial contribution to climate change mitigation". For a patrimonial owner this means higher residual value, lower exposure to future penalties on energy-obsolete assets and real differentiation against conventional residential stock.

At an individual scale, this framework explains why PAPIK applies the same level of technical demand to a family commission and to a patrimonial commission: design and material decisions that today look like an extra cost become, within a decade, the foundation of the asset's long-term value.

What you can require from a sustainable project

If you are evaluating a new build or a retrofit and want to make sure the sustainability dimension is real and not decorative, five questions separate a technical project from a brochure project.

The first is whether the project provides an LCA calculation under EN 15978, performed with a recognised tool (PHPP, OneClick LCA, WUFI, EnergyPlus) and expressed in kgCO₂eq/m². The second is whether the principal materials (structure, insulation, windows) come with current EPDs. The third is whether the timber is certified FSC or PEFC with documented chain of custody. The fourth is whether the building targets a verifiable formal certification (Passivhaus PHI, EnerPHit on retrofits, LEED, BREEAM) or simply markets itself as a "sustainable home" without verifiable commitment. The fifth is whether the project assumes a fifty- or hundred-year durability horizon, with constructive detailing consistent with that horizon.

If all five answers are solid, the project has real sustainability. If three or more are missing, what you have is advertising.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between an "efficient home" and a "sustainable home"?

An efficient home is one that uses little energy in operation. A sustainable home considers every phase: materials, construction, use, end of life. Every well-designed Passivhaus is efficient, but it is sustainable only if it is paired with consistent material and durability decisions.

Does an LCA add a lot to the project cost?

If it is integrated from the early design stage, the cost is modest and is often offset by better material decisions. A detailed LCA with third-party verification has a higher cost and is only necessary for specific certifications (LEED, BREEAM, advanced European taxonomy reporting).

Can an existing home be retrofitted to Passivhaus standards?

Yes. The EnerPHit scheme from the Passivhaus Institute is the specific retrofit framework, with requirements adapted to the realities of existing fabric. It can take buildings from the 1970s or 1980s very close to new-build Passivhaus performance and is the natural route for assets you want to keep relevant long term.

Are biobased materials safe in case of fire?

Insulations such as blown cellulose or sprayed cork have certified fire performance under European regulations (Euroclasses) and, when combined with boards such as Fermacell or gypsum plasterboard, deliver protection equivalent to or better than traditional mineral systems. Each product's certification documents this specifically.

How can I verify a builder's environmental claims?

By requesting formal certifications (Passivhaus PHI, EnerPHit, FSC, PEFC, LEED, BREEAM), EPDs for the principal materials, an LCA calculation under EN 15978 and the result of the airtightness (Blower Door) test. If all of that is missing, the claims are advertising.
