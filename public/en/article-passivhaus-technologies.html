Five key Passivhaus technologies for 2026

Continuous insulation, airtightness, ventilation with heat recovery, high-efficiency windows and integrated home automation. The five technologies that define a Passivhaus home in 2026.

Continuous thermal insulation

Continuous insulation eliminates the cold (or warm, depending on the season) zones generated when an insulating material has interruptions or bridges. In a conventional house, the structure inevitably creates these interruptions: columns, beams, corners, construction joints. In Passivhaus, the envelope is a continuous, uninterrupted layer, which requires a specific construction approach.

Technically, this implies thicknesses greater than those of a basic CTE (Spanish Technical Building Code) build, typically between 18 and 30 centimetres, as well as geometric continuity verified with detailed construction drawings and materials suited to the local climate (cellulose, high-density mineral wool, cork, EPS or XPS depending on the zone). The target U-value is usually below 0.15 W/m²K in walls and below 0.12 W/m²K in roofs.

At PAPIK we routinely use blown-in cellulose for its combination of recycled origin, thermal performance and hygroscopicity, an option we cover in detail in the article on sustainable materials.

Controlled airtightness

Airtightness is the envelope's capacity to block uncontrolled air infiltration. A conventional house can have an involuntary air-change rate (n50) of between 10 and 15 h⁻¹. A certified Passivhaus requires n50 below 0.6 h⁻¹, which makes it roughly twenty times more airtight than a standard build.

Airtightness is verified with the blower-door test, which depressurises the dwelling and measures the air leakage. If the result fails to meet the threshold, the house is not certified as Passivhaus.

The technologies that make this possible are specific: airtight membranes (kraft paper, specialised polymeric sheets) between structure and finish, dedicated adhesive tapes for joints, gaskets and service penetrations, airtight boxes for sockets and switches on exterior walls, and rigorous construction details at risk points such as wall-to-roof junctions, windows and service penetrations. Insufficient airtightness nullifies most of the rest of the investment, which is why blower-door verification is part of the standard quality control process on every Passivhaus site.

Mechanical ventilation with heat recovery (MVHR)

An airtight dwelling requires controlled mechanical ventilation. Without it, the indoor air deteriorates and humidity problems arise. Mechanical ventilation with heat recovery solves this dilemma by renewing the air constantly without significant energy loss.

The operation is as follows: one fan extracts stale air from bathrooms, kitchen and wet rooms, while another supplies fresh air to the main rooms (living rooms, bedrooms). Between the two air streams, a heat exchanger transfers the energy from the outgoing air to the incoming air without the two mixing. Typical recovery efficiency sits between 85% and 92% depending on the system used.

The practical result is that you have constantly renewed air at room temperature, without having to open windows in winter, with the energy loss that would entail, or in summer, with the consequent heat ingress. At PAPIK we routinely use Zehnder systems, the European benchmark in residential MVHR, sized according to the dwelling's geometry and the projected number of occupants.

High-performance windows

Windows are the thermal weak point of any envelope. In a conventional house, a window may have a U-value of between 2.5 and 3.5 W/m²K. In a Passivhaus, the target is a U-value below 0.8 W/m²K, which makes them three to four times more insulating than conventional windows.

The technology that makes this possible combines several elements: triple glazing with inert gas chambers (argon or krypton) between panes, low-e (low emissivity) coatings on the glass to reduce thermal radiation, thermally broken spacers between panes instead of the traditional metallic ones, insulated frames (typically wood, high-end PVC, aluminium with thermal break, or hybrid systems), and installation with airtight tapes integrated with the envelope to guarantee continuity around the perimeter.

Orientation also matters in a structural sense. Windows facing south, in the Catalan climate, contribute more solar energy than they lose and act as energy assets. North-facing windows are net losses and must be sized carefully so as not to compromise the overall performance.

Home automation and integrated systems

The fifth technology is not a material, but the intelligent integration of all the home's systems. A modern Passivhaus dwelling typically incorporates temperature, CO₂ and humidity sensors distributed across the spaces, an integrated management system (Loxone, KNX or equivalent) that adjusts climate control, ventilation and solar shading according to conditions, integrated photovoltaic generation with priority management (self-consumption, battery charging, grid injection), a self-consumption battery for continuity in case of supply interruptions (see the case of the 2025 blackout), an air-source heat pump (aerothermal) for climate control and domestic hot water managed according to solar production, and an electric vehicle charger with priority management.

Home automation adds between 10,000 and 30,000 euros to the budget depending on the level of integration, and is not essential for Passivhaus certification. Its inclusion is justified when the client seeks to multiply the energy performance and comfort of the home beyond what the construction itself already delivers by design.

How PAPIK integrates them into the Eskimohaus® system

PAPIK's Eskimohaus® construction system is the result of combining these five technologies with materials and methods adapted to the Catalan climate. The works incorporate a lightweight timber frame structure with continuous cellulose or cork insulation; airtightness verified with a post-build blower-door test; a Zehnder MVHR sized to the project; triple-glazed windows with wood or hybrid frames; and home automation integrated with photovoltaic generation and an optional battery.

The operational result is homes that meet the Passivhaus standard with costs compatible with the Catalan mid-to-upper residential market, verifiable durability in excess of one hundred years, and constant thermal comfort throughout the year with no oscillations perceptible to the occupant.

What all of this means for the end client

A house with the five technologies properly integrated consumes up to 90% less than an equivalent conventional build, holds the indoor temperature within a narrow range of 19 to 25 degrees throughout the year (with minimal intervention from climate control systems), and renews the indoor air constantly without draughts or sudden changes. The dwelling is less vulnerable to power outages, since essential systems can be sustained on self-consumption and battery. The structural service life is verifiable beyond one hundred years, and the market revaluation is higher, between 15% and 25% more than an equivalent class E or F build, according to studies from the real estate sector (Sociedad de Tasación, Idealista Data).

Frequently asked questions

Do you need Passivhaus certification to enjoy these technologies?

No. The technologies deliver energy and comfort value regardless of formal certification. The Passivhaus certification from the Darmstadt Institute is an additional decision for the client, since it adds processing cost but provides third-party validation.

Can MVHR be incorporated into a retrofit?

Yes. Retrofitting with MVHR is common on EnerPHit projects (Passivhaus for retrofit). It requires planning of ductwork and suitable placement of the main unit during the design phase.

What type of windows do you recommend?

It depends on the desired aesthetics for the project. Wood frames or wood-aluminium hybrids are usually the best combination of performance, aesthetics and durability for residential Passivhaus work in the Mediterranean climate.

Is home automation essential?

No. A Passivhaus home can function perfectly without home automation. Home automation adds optimisation and comfort, but it is not a requirement of the standard.
