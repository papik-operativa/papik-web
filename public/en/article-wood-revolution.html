The Timber Revolution: Why We Build with Timber and Where Mass Timber Is Heading

Timber as a Sustainable Structural Material

An honest evaluation of a material's environmental performance requires looking at the full life cycle according to EN 15978 and a complete Life Cycle Assessment (LCA). When this exercise is performed for reinforced concrete and for structural laminated timber, the difference is substantial. Manufacturing one cubic metre of reinforced concrete releases between 250 and 400 kg of CO₂, depending on dosage and the percentage of Portland cement. Manufacturing one cubic metre of CLT, by contrast, has a negative carbon footprint: the tree has captured roughly 800 kg of CO₂ during its growth, and that carbon remains fixed in the structure for as long as the building exists.

The argument is not limited to emissions accounting. Timber with FSC or PEFC certification comes from forests managed with a felling rate lower than the growth rate, which in some European territories is contributing to the expansion of forest cover rather than its retreat. Regenerative forestry is, at present, the only primary production system in the construction sector that adds ecological value to the territory rather than extracting it.

CLT and Mass Timber: The Industrial Leap

The term mass timber covers three industrial systems that have transformed what is possible to build in timber: cross-laminated timber (CLT), glue-laminated timber (glulam) and nail-laminated timber (NLT). Of the three, CLT has reached the highest level of maturity in the European market and is the system that has enabled the leap in height of timber buildings.

CLT consists of panels manufactured from alternating layers of sawn timber laid in perpendicular directions and bonded under industrial pressure. The result is a structural element with load-bearing capacity equivalent to reinforced concrete for the same thickness, with a density roughly five times lower and millimetric manufacturing tolerance. Panels arrive on site prefabricated with openings, service penetrations and fixings already resolved, which reduces the structural execution timeline to a fraction of what a conventional system requires.

At PAPIK we use CLT mainly in projects where thermal mass and combined structural performance justify the additional cost compared with light timber framing. The selection is made case by case. Not every project requires it and not every budget admits it. When applied, however, the combination of structural CLT with cellulose insulation as the thermal layer and cork or wood fibre as the outer layer produces envelopes with exceptional thermal performance and a carbon footprint that most conventional systems cannot match.

Carbon Sequestration: The Double Count

One of the most common confusions in the sector is to treat timber's carbon sequestration as if it were a marketing claim. It is not. It is a quantifiable and auditable operation.

Each cubic metre of structural timber contains roughly 250 kg of atmospheric carbon captured by the tree during growth, equivalent to 0.9 tonnes of CO₂ retained per cubic metre. A 200 m² single-family home built with mass timber structure can incorporate between 30 and 50 tonnes of CO₂ fixed in the building throughout its useful life, typically fifty to one hundred years. If at some point the building is dismantled and the timber reused, the carbon remains sequestered. If the timber ends up in landfill, it is released. End-of-life management is therefore a legitimate part of LCA evaluation and a point where PAPIK applies specific demountability criteria.

The combined effect is what the technical literature calls structural substitution: for every cubic metre of concrete or steel that is not manufactured and is replaced by laminated timber, the difference in avoided emissions plus captured carbon ranges between one and two tonnes of CO₂ equivalent. At the scale of a multi-family building the figure reaches hundreds of tonnes.

Timber and the Mediterranean Climate

One of the most frequent questions we receive is whether timber works well in our climate, where the hot dry summer is the principal challenge and seasonal humidity can be demanding. The answer is yes, with some nuance.

The low thermal mass of timber allows the home to respond quickly to temperature changes, which can be exploited through passive solar shading and night-time ventilation strategies. The intrinsic insulating capacity of timber (around 0.13 W/mK thermal conductivity) is between five and ten times higher than that of brick or concrete, which substantially reduces the insulation thickness required to meet the Spanish Building Code (CTE) and Passivhaus targets. The hygroscopic behaviour of timber absorbs water vapour when the environment is humid and releases it when dry, contributing to stable interior comfort without the need for active dehumidifiers.

At the K-Valld'Or project of 280 m², thermal simulations confirmed that light timber frame walls with 20 cm of wood fibre insulation kept the interior temperature stable through summer heatwaves without active cooling for most of the year. The combination of low envelope thermal mass with targeted interior thermal mass (block separation wall, lime mortar floor) allows the thermal response of the assembly to be tuned with precision and without resorting to structural concrete.

Fire Behaviour and Durability

The most frequent question we receive from owners is whether timber burns. The honest technical answer is yes, but the fire behaviour of a mass timber structure is predictable, slow and calculable, which cannot be said of structural steel, which loses load-bearing capacity abruptly once critical temperature is exceeded.

A CLT or glulam beam, in a fire, chars at the surface at a known rate (approximately 0.7 mm per minute) and retains its structural integrity for a time that can be calculated with precision. Our structures meet the fire resistance requirements of the Spanish Building Code (CTE), R-60 or R-90 depending on the case, through oversized sections and the use of plasterboard as passive protection. In a Passivhaus, ventilation is mechanical and controlled, there are no draughts feeding a fire, and ventilation systems include automatic fire dampers.

Durability is the other point often questioned. A correctly designed timber structure, with appropriate constructive protections (separation from the ground, ventilated cavities, end-grain protection) and certified FSC or PEFC timber, has a useful life of more than one hundred years. There are documented timber buildings over five hundred years old. Timber is not a fragile material. It is a material that requires constructive knowledge, like every good material.

Forestry Management and Certification

The environmental argument for timber holds only if the timber comes from regenerative forestry. Buying uncertified timber is equivalent to giving up the entire carbon balance described in the previous sections. PAPIK works exclusively with timber certified by FSC (Forest Stewardship Council) or PEFC (Programme for the Endorsement of Forest Certification), the two international systems that verify full traceability from forest to site.

FSC certification guarantees that the felling rate is lower than the forest growth rate, that biodiversity in forest ecosystems is preserved, that workers' and local communities' rights are respected, and that documentary traceability exists for every batch. PEFC certification operates with equivalent criteria and, in the European context, covers a slightly larger forest area. Both systems are acceptable and our selection is based on regional availability of each species and the origin of the supplier.

At PAPIK we mainly use spruce and pine of European origin (Austria, Germany, Scandinavia), species with excellent strength-to-weight ratio and short logistic chains that limit transport footprint. For exterior applications we use Accoya or certified native Mediterranean timbers, depending on the project.

The European Regulatory Context

Recent amendments to the Spanish Building Code (CTE) and to Eurocode 5 have made the calculation and approval of mass timber buildings easier across Spain. Countries such as Sweden, Austria, Switzerland, the United Kingdom and Canada have developed specific regulations that allow timber buildings up to eighteen storeys, and the European Commission is working on common regulatory frameworks that will harmonise practice at continental scale.

In Spain the path is still being built, but the first multi-family residential buildings with CLT structure are already operational and projects in planning multiply year after year. Regulatory inertia is real and the technical training of the sector is still uneven, but the direction is unequivocal.

Lessons From Fifteen Years of Timber Construction

Our field experience allows us to extract some practical conclusions of value to any owner considering a timber construction project.

The first is that the quality of the timber matters more than the system. The same mass timber structure can perform excellently or poorly depending on the origin, drying and quality control of the product. Working with suppliers with complete data sheets, documented use class and current certification is the first preventive measure.

The second is that prefabrication improves the outcome. When structural elements arrive on site with factory machining, execution errors are drastically reduced and the thermal and acoustic performance of the envelope improves. The millimetric precision of CLT is, in many projects, the factor that allows reaching 0.3 ACH on the Blower Door test, well below the Passivhaus limit of 0.6.

The third is that durability is built into the details. Wall bases separated from the ground, properly sized cavity ventilation, end-grain protection at exposed extremities and correct rainwater management are the points where a good timber structure differs from a mediocre one. None of this costs significantly more. It all depends on the knowledge of the designer and the contractor.

The fourth is that cost is not always the decisive factor. CLT timber has a cost similar to reinforced concrete for equivalent structures when total costs are compared, including foundations (significantly lighter), timelines (much shorter) and life-cycle operations. The initial investment may be 5 to 10 per cent higher, but the total cost balance over fifty years often tilts the decision toward timber.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is CLT more expensive than reinforced concrete?

It depends on the project. In single-family construction the initial cost is often 5 to 15 per cent higher. In multi-family buildings the balance can equalise and even tilt toward timber, especially when the project takes advantage of execution speed and savings on foundations. An indicative technical evaluation is the best way to answer the question case by case.

What useful life does a mass timber structure have?

One hundred years as a minimum, with normal maintenance. There are documented historic timber buildings over five hundred years old. Real useful life depends more on the constructive design (water protections, cavity ventilation) than on the material itself.

What is the difference between CLT, glulam and NLT?

CLT are panels of cross-laid layers used for walls and floors. Glulam are laminated beams used for linear structures (columns, beams). NLT are panels of nail-laminated rather than glued timber, more artisanal and with slightly lower performance. All three are part of what is known as mass timber.

Does exterior timber need painting or treatment every few years?

It depends on the timber type and treatment. Accoya timber or some well-detailed native species can last thirty to fifty years without structural intervention, only with occasional aesthetic maintenance. Untreated timber changes colour over time (silver grey) without compromising integrity.

CLOSING

The timber revolution is not a distant promise of the future. It is the material with which we have built our last hundred and twenty plus homes and the material we will continue to build with. The industrial maturity of CLT, the availability of FSC and PEFC certified timber, and quantifiable carbon sequestration make timber the structural option most coherent with the climate present and the regulatory future of the sector.
