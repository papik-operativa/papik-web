Three houses with power on during the 2025 blackout

K-Codines, K-Vall d'Or and K-Llavaneres kept electricity running during the Iberian grid failure of May 2025. What these Passivhaus houses have in common.

What happened on 28 April 2025

The 28 April incident was a generalised failure of the Iberian electrical system that affected Spain and Portugal for an extended period. Subsequent media coverage, including reports in El Confidencial Digital, Majadahonda Magazín and Hechos de Hoy, highlighted the vulnerability of the grid to events of this magnitude and the operational difficulty of restoring supply rapidly across such an extensive area.

For most homes, the blackout meant several hours without climate control, without lighting, with health risks linked to loss of refrigeration and the operation of dependent medical equipment, and with loss of connectivity. For three houses built by PAPIK, the day was noticeably different.

What K-Codines, K-Vall d'Or and K-Llavaneres have in common

All three homes share a constructive and installation pattern that allows them to operate with partial autonomy in the face of supply cuts. The specific combination varies according to project, but the structural elements coincide.

The construction system is the first factor. The three houses use the Eskimohaus® system with a lightweight frame, continuous insulation, n50 airtightness verified with a blower-door test, and ventilation with heat recovery. The direct consequence of this combination is that the house maintains its interior temperature for hours without the need for active climate control. If electricity fails, the indoor temperature does not collapse immediately.

In-house energy production is the second factor. Each home incorporates photovoltaic panels sized to cover basic consumption. During daylight hours, the photovoltaic system continues to produce energy despite the grid failure, provided the installation is prepared to operate in island mode (see the next section).

Self-consumption with battery storage is the decisive element. When the grid fails, the battery takes over automatically and powers essential loads: lighting, refrigerator, communications, critical climate-control systems. The transition is transparent to the occupants, who often do not immediately notice that the grid has gone down.

K-Codines is a particular case within the group, because it is conceived as a self-sufficient home in off-grid mode, not merely grid-ready. K-Vall d'Or and K-Llavaneres operate connected to the grid but with a partial islanding capacity that activates automatically.

Why these three and not others

In Catalonia there are thousands of homes with photovoltaic panels. Most of them, however, do not work when the grid fails. The technical reason is well known in the electrical sector but poorly communicated to the general public, which is why many owners discover this fact precisely during a cut.

A photovoltaic installation connected to a conventional grid, for regulatory safety reasons, automatically disconnects when it detects a voltage drop. This anti-islanding protection prevents technicians working on grid restoration from receiving voltage from houses with active photovoltaics, and it is essential from the standpoint of operator safety.

To maintain consumption during a cut, the system requires an inverter with hybrid functionality (which detects the drop and activates autonomous battery mode) and an electrical architecture prepared for island operation, with critical circuits segregated from the rest of the installation. K-Codines, K-Vall d'Or and K-Llavaneres have this architecture by design from the project phase, not as a subsequent add-on. This is the structural difference between a conventional photovoltaic installation and one prepared for autonomy.

What we learn from this case

The 28 April blackout was an extreme case, but not unique. Supply cuts are increasing in Catalonia and Europe due to several factors: grid saturation from the accelerated energy transition, more frequent extreme weather events, insufficient maintenance of existing infrastructure, and growing cyber-vulnerability of the electrical system to digital threats.

Building a house with partial autonomy ceases to be a technical caprice and becomes a reasonable decision in residential risk management. The elements that define this autonomy are five: a quality thermal envelope that does not require continuous climate control, in-house production with photovoltaic panels correctly sized to real consumption, a self-consumption battery with capacity for critical operation of six to twenty-four hours, a hybrid inverter capable of automatic transition between grid and island mode, and an electrical architecture with segregated critical circuits that can operate even when the main grid is down.

What you can do if you want this kind of protection

If you are building or retrofitting a home and want to incorporate energy autonomy against cuts, proper planning depends on the moment of the project. In new-build, it is essential to specify the requirement from the project phase. Incorporating photovoltaics and battery is substantially cheaper and more efficient if planned at the initial design stage rather than added later, and our Construction and Development units consider it as a standard option.

In existing homes it is possible through energy retrofit, including the installation of photovoltaics and battery, replacement of the inverter with a hybrid model, and segregation of critical circuits to allow island operation. This intervention is compatible with Next Generation EU subsidies under programmes 4 and 5, which can significantly reduce the net cost for the owner.

Frequently asked questions

What battery capacity do I need to cover a 12-hour cut?

It depends on the essential consumption of the home. A battery of five to ten kWh typically covers eight to twenty-four hours of critical consumption (lighting, refrigerator, communications, minimal climate control). Optimal sizing is assessed case by case during the project phase according to the projected real consumption profile.

Does photovoltaics work during a blackout?

Only if the inverter is hybrid and the installation is prepared for island operation with segregated critical circuits. Conventional installations automatically disconnect for regulatory safety reasons.

Compatible with NGEU subsidies?

Yes. NGEU programme 4 finances self-consumption and battery storage up to significant percentages of the cost. PAPIK manages the procedure end-to-end in energy-retrofit projects, and in some cases integrates it into new-build projects when the client meets the specific requirements of the programme.

Can an existing home be retrofitted for this autonomy?

Yes, through retrofit. The cost depends on the current state of the electrical installation and of the envelope. An integral retrofit is the optimal moment to incorporate it, as it allows planning the segregation of critical circuits simultaneously with the rest of the interventions.
