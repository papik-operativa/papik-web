## INTRO

The Passivhaus standard has existed since the nineties. Its core methodology (continuous insulation, controlled airtightness, ventilation with heat recovery, elimination of thermal bridges) has consolidated as an international benchmark. What has not stopped evolving are the technologies that materialise these principles, and their level of sophistication in 2026 is substantially higher than a decade ago.

This article identifies five technical innovations that are transforming Passivhaus construction today and describes how they integrate into PAPIK's Eskimohaus® building system. Each innovation has its optimal scope of application, and not all of them fit every project.

---

## H2 · 1. Hygrovariable membranes

Hygrovariable membranes are vapour barriers with variable permeability depending on ambient humidity. Unlike conventional barriers, these membranes block water vapour from passing from the interior into the insulation during winter, when the interior is humid, and allow vapour to pass from the insulation back into the interior during summer, when the interior is dry.

The result is a thermal envelope that self-regulates against seasonal humidity, preventing interstitial condensation problems that can degrade insulation over time. In Catalonia, where summers are warm and humid and winters are cold and dry, this technology is particularly well suited compared to other European climates.

---

## H2 · 2. Phase Change Materials (PCM)

PCMs (Phase Change Materials) are materials that absorb and release thermal energy when changing state (solid to liquid) at a specific temperature, usually around twenty or twenty-five degrees Celsius. Integrated into panels, plasterboard or microcapsules within mortars, PCMs absorb heat when the indoor temperature rises during the day (solid-to-liquid change), and release heat when the temperature drops at night (liquid-to-solid change).

The practical effect is added thermal inertia in lightweight construction. Traditionally, lightweight building systems such as timber framing have less thermal inertia than massive construction, which can generate sharper indoor temperature swings. PCMs offset this limitation, maintaining a more stable indoor temperature with less daily fluctuation.

At PAPIK we incorporate PCMs selectively in projects that require it, typically in orientations with high summer solar exposure or in interior zones at risk of thermal stress.

---

## H2 · 3. BIPV: Building Integrated Photovoltaics

BIPV (Building Integrated Photovoltaics) means installing photovoltaics as part of the building, rather than adding them on top of the roof as a stand-alone element. The main types are photovoltaic tiles that replace conventional tiles and generate energy, ventilated photovoltaic facades using PV panels as cladding, and photovoltaic glass integrated into windows or curtain walls with built-in generation.

The main advantage is aesthetic and regulatory: the system disappears as a visible element because it is the skin of the building, allowing the use of surfaces that would otherwise do nothing (south-facing facades, roof areas not usable with conventional panels) while remaining compatible with restrictive landscape ordinances. This last point is particularly relevant in municipalities such as Matadepera or Sitges, where ordinances limit visible elements on the roof or facade.

In return, BIPV has a higher cost per kWp installed than conventional photovoltaics. It makes sense in specific projects where aesthetic or regulatory demands justify the additional cost, and not in standard residential projects where a conventional rooftop installation would be equally valid.

## H2 · 4. Industrial prefabrication

Prefabricating building elements in a workshop, rather than on site, is transforming the speed and quality of Passivhaus construction. The most common prefabricated elements are Passivhaus walls, manufactured with lightweight framing and integrated insulation in the workshop, transported and assembled on site; complete bathroom units (sanitary fixtures, services, finishes) prefabricated and installed on site as a sealed module; and prefabricated kitchens with cabinetry, finishes and integrated services.

The benefits are threefold. Quality is controlled in the workshop, with stable environmental conditions and standardised quality control, reducing the construction defects typically associated with on-site work exposed to the elements. Construction timelines are significantly compressed, since on-site assembly is much faster than full in-situ construction. Waste generation on site is lower, as industrial manufacturing handles waste in the workshop with greater efficiency.

At PAPIK we combine prefabricated elements with on-site construction depending on the project. Full prefabrication is viable for some standard residential projects, but for unique single-family homes on complex plots, the mixed approach (on-site structure with prefabricated bathrooms and kitchens) is often more efficient.

## H2 · 5. AI-assisted design and advanced energy simulation

The fifth axis of innovation is not a material, but a methodology. Advanced energy simulation software such as PHPP, WUFI Plus, DesignBuilder or EnergyPlus today allows the thermal behaviour of a building to be modelled before it is built with a precision unthinkable a decade ago.

Combined with optimisation tools assisted by artificial intelligence, these software packages allow optimisation of orientation, shading, glazing and insulation according to cost-benefit parameters, simulation of behaviour across all 8,760 hours of the year using real local climate data, and detection of inflection points where a specific construction decision has a disproportionately high impact on final performance.

For a PAPIK Passivhaus project, prior simulation is standard, not optional. What has changed compared to a decade ago is the speed and precision of iterations: today dozens of alternatives can be tested in hours rather than days, and this allows the project to be optimised before reaching the execution phase.

## H2 · 6. What this all means for the end client

A client who builds a home with PAPIK in 2026 incorporates, often without realising it, several of these innovations. The envelope uses membranes appropriate to the local climate; you may receive a PCM proposal if orientation or programme justifies it; the photovoltaics may be conventional or BIPV depending on the project's aesthetics and applicable ordinances; specific elements (bathrooms, kitchen modules) may be prefabricated to improve quality and timelines; and the project is fully simulated before construction, not adjusted on the fly during the works.

PAPIK's goal is not to incorporate technology for the sake of fashion. It is to offer the best cost-benefit-performance trade-off for each specific case, which is why not all innovations are applied to every project, and the appropriate combination is defined during the preliminary design phase.

## H2 · 7. Frequently asked questions

### Do you need to incorporate all these innovations to achieve Passivhaus?

No. The Passivhaus standard is achieved through the core methodology (insulation, airtightness, ventilation, thermal bridges). Additional innovations serve to optimise costs, integrate aesthetics or add specific features in projects that justify them.

### Is BIPV more expensive than conventional photovoltaics?

Yes, usually. The additional cost is assessed case by case based on the project's aesthetic requirements and applicable ordinances. In projects in areas such as Sitges or Matadepera, where ordinances limit visible elements on the roof, the BIPV premium can be justified by regulatory compatibility.

### Does prefabrication reduce construction quality?

On the contrary. Workshop fabrication allows greater quality control than on-site work exposed to the elements, with standardised processes and controlled environmental conditions. The key is the right combination between prefabricated elements and professional on-site assembly.
