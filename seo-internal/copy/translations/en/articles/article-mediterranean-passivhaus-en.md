Mediterranean Passivhaus, the cooling-first paradigm

## INTRO

The Passivhaus standard was developed in 1990 in Darmstadt by the engineer Wolfgang Feist in the context of a continental cold climate, where the operational priority is to minimise heating demand. That origin has shaped public perception of the standard as a method "for making warm houses". In the Mediterranean climate the reality is the opposite: the dominant problem for seven months of the year is heat, not cold. Building Passivhaus in Catalonia, the Balearic Islands or Andorra requires a specific adaptation that inverts the design priorities. This article describes what that adaptation consists of, which technologies change and how PAPIK applies it within the Eskimohaus® system.

---

## H2 · 1. Why the German Passivhaus does not transfer directly

In Frankfurt or Darmstadt, the average January temperature sits around zero degrees, while the July average barely reaches twenty. Annual heating demand far exceeds cooling demand: if you size the home to minimise winter losses, summer is essentially solved by default. In the Catalan climate, by contrast, the July and August averages in Sant Cugat del Vallès sit between twenty-four and twenty-seven degrees, with daily peaks routinely above thirty and, during heat events, sustained for several days at thirty-five or more. The summer indoor-outdoor gradient is of comparable magnitude to the winter one, and a Passivhaus designed with the original logic tends to overheat.

The origin of the problem is physical: a well-insulated, airtight envelope conserves heat regardless of its sign. In winter it protects interior energy from the exterior cold; in summer, if heat gets in (through poorly protected windows, reverse thermal bridges, deficient night-time ventilation), the envelope conserves it exactly the same way. The home becomes a thermos flask, and a thermos flask with heat inside does not cool down easily.

---

## H2 · 2. Cooling-first as a design principle

The first paradigm shift is to invert the design hierarchy. Instead of "minimise winter losses and then see how summer resolves itself", the Mediterranean logic starts from "minimise summer gains and then see how winter resolves itself". The operational result usually matches the German Passivhaus in insulation and airtightness, but differs significantly in four areas: orientation, shading, ventilation and thermal mass.

This inversion is not aesthetic but numerical. The annual load balance calculated with PHPP (Passive House Planning Package, the official tool of the Darmstadt Institute) shows that in the Mediterranean climate the cooling demand can match or exceed the heating demand. A well-designed project trims both without having to oversize any active system.

---

## H2 · 3. Structural shading of the southern orientation

In Germany, south-facing windows are energy assets: they contribute more solar energy than they lose over the year. In Catalonia, that reading holds in winter but inverts in summer. An unprotected south window can deliver one thousand watts per square metre at midday in July; multiplied by a generous area, it becomes a disproportionate heat ingress.

The solution is calibrated structural shading. Overhangs sized to the local solar trajectory (at the latitude of Barcelona, an overhang of approximately one metre over a window of two metres blocks the summer sun but admits the winter sun), adjustable exterior shutters, movable louvres, vegetated pergolas with deciduous plants that shade in summer and let the sun through in winter. The usual design rule is: every square metre of south window needs its own exterior shading system, not only an interior one. An interior blind blocks the radiation but the heat has already entered through the glass.

---

## H2 · 4. Controlled daytime ventilation and free night-time ventilation

MVHR (mechanical ventilation with heat recovery), as set out for cold climates, works perfectly in Catalonia during winter. In summer, however, performance shifts. The system continues to renew the indoor air with the usual filtration and quality, but two additional strategies combine with MVHR.

The first is the summer bypass: most Zehnder systems and equivalents incorporate an automatic bypass that, when the outdoor temperature drops below the indoor (typically from eleven at night in Catalonia), introduces outdoor air without passing through the heat exchanger, cooling the home passively. The second is cross night-time ventilation: opening windows on opposite sides during the hours of outdoor minimum and closing them first thing in the morning, cooling the home's thermal mass with the cool dawn air.

In practice, a well-designed Mediterranean Passivhaus home can hold indoor temperatures between twenty-three and twenty-six degrees throughout the summer without active air conditioning, thanks to the combination of shading, bypass and night-time ventilation. We detail this in the article on [Passivhaus ventilation](/en/article-passivhaus-ventilation).

## H2 · 5. Thermal mass as a regulator

The classic German Passivhaus is often built with a lightweight structure (timber frame) and little thermal mass. In the Mediterranean climate, thermal mass is a first-order design asset. A home with concrete slabs, internal partitions of ceramic block, internal renders or clay finishes absorbs heat during the peak summer hours and releases it slowly through the night, smoothing the daytime oscillations that would affect a lightweight build.

At PAPIK we typically combine a lightweight timber-frame structure on the external envelope (light, fast to execute and with low embodied carbon) with thermal mass concentrated in strategic interior walls and slabs. This combination, which echoes Mediterranean construction traditions (the Catalan or Mallorcan rural house is, historically, an example of well-used thermal mass), is technically compatible with the Eskimohaus® system and contributes measurably to summer comfort.

## H2 · 6. Hybrid active cooling when needed

In extreme events or on difficult orientations, passive cooling may prove insufficient. The Mediterranean strategy integrates hybrid active cooling: reversible air-source heat pump (aerothermal) with reduced sizing (between two and four kilowatts for a Passivhaus home of one hundred and eighty square metres, against the five to eight kilowatts of an equivalent conventional build), intelligent control that prioritises cooling with daytime photovoltaic generation and manages night-time ventilation automatically, and, optionally, radiant cooling floor using the same hydraulic installation that in winter provides heating.

The objective is not to eliminate active cooling but to reduce it to a marginal complement to passive cooling. The annual electrical consumption associated with cooling in a well-designed Mediterranean Passivhaus typically sits between two and six kilowatt-hours per square metre, against the twenty to forty of a conventional build.

---

## H2 · 7. Materials adapted to the local climate

Beyond structure and thermal mass, two specific material elements have design relevance in the Mediterranean climate. The first is light exterior finishes, which reflect rather than absorb radiation: whites, light greys or light earth tones, characteristic of the Balearic and Empordà architectural tradition, reduce surface temperature by up to eight degrees compared with dark finishes. The second is hygroscopic interior finishes (clay, lime, wood without synthetic varnish), which stabilise indoor humidity and, indirectly, improve thermal sensation without needing to lower the temperature further.

These materials are not exclusive to Mediterranean Passivhaus, but their coherent integration with cooling-first logic and thermal mass is part of what distinguishes Passivhaus adapted to the Catalan climate from a simple import of German standards. See the detail in the article on [sustainable materials](/en/article-sustainable-materials).

## H2 · 8. Frequently asked questions

### Is there a Passivhaus certification specific to the Mediterranean climate?

The Darmstadt Institute does not issue a geographically differentiated certification, but the PHPP methodology recalculates every variable according to the local climatic data entered. A Passivhaus home in Sant Cugat del Vallès is certified under the same rules as one in Darmstadt, but the load balance is structurally different.

### What cost difference does adapting it to the Mediterranean climate involve?

The adaptation does not translate into a significant additional cost when planned in design. Overhangs and shading integrated into the design cost less than adding them retroactively. Thermal mass is solved with standard construction materials. Hybrid active cooling typically replaces part of the investment in conventional systems.

### Can a home with abundant natural light be Mediterranean Passivhaus?

Yes. The rule is not to reduce windows but to protect them. A project with large south openings is perfectly compatible with cooling-first if the exterior shading is sized accordingly. Clients who prioritise natural light do not need to give it up to meet the standard.

### Does Passivhaus work in the Balearic Islands or Andorra?

Yes, in both cases. In the Balearic Islands the Mediterranean logic is accentuated. In Andorra, at altitude, the regime is closer to the original continental, and the heating-first approach becomes dominant again. PAPIK adapts sizing to each location through the specific PHPP calculation.
