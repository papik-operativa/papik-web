## INTRO

Calculating the construction budget of a Passivhaus home is more complex than applying a generic €/m² rate. The plot, the architectural design, the permits, the construction system, the finishes and several operational factors all play a role, and these last items often fall outside the first estimate. The usual consequence is that budget comparisons between builders arrive with significant differences not because of the apparent factors, but because of what each estimate includes or excludes.

This guide breaks down the components that define the real cost of a Passivhaus (high-performance certified low-energy) home in Catalonia, based on public sector ranges (ITeC, COAC, IDAE; Catalan, Catalan and Spanish technical and energy agencies) and on PAPIK's accumulated experience building more than one hundred projects. Specific figures from internal projects are not published here: the personalised estimate for each client's particular case is generated through the [budget configurator](/en/budget).

---

## H2 · 1. Plot

The plot is the first cost in any construction project, and often the most underestimated in early budget approximations. In Catalonia, the price of residential land varies widely depending on several factors. The municipality and the area within the municipality are the primary determinant: Sant Cugat, Bellaterra and Matadepera sit in the high ranges; Sant Quirze, Castellar or Castelldefels in mid-high ranges; more peripheral municipalities in mid-low ranges.

The buildable potential of the plot is the second factor: the same plot may have very different construction potential depending on the applicable POUM (municipal planning ordinance), and this is reflected proportionally in the market price. Topography matters significantly, since a plot with a steep slope generates additional excavation and foundation costs that can reach tens of thousands of euros. Available utilities also have an impact: if water, electricity or sewerage are missing, bringing in these services may add significant items to the construction budget.

If you do not yet have a plot, PAPIK's self-development department can guide the search through a network of established real estate partners across the territory, as part of the [Construction](/en/construction) service.

## H2 · 2. Architectural and technical design

The design is the intellectual foundation of the project. It covers the preliminary design (initial concept and typology decisions), the basic design (volumes, layout, general definition), the executive design (construction details, specifications, working drawings), the building services design (electrical, plumbing, climate control, ventilation, photovoltaics), the energy efficiency study with PHPP (Passive House Planning Package) for Passivhaus certification, and the health and safety study.

At PAPIK, the design is developed by the in-house architecture department as part of the Construction process. If you already work with an external architect, the coordination between studio and builder is resolved during the preliminary design phase, with no duplication of functions.

---

## H2 · 3. Planning permits and fees

Municipal fees for major works are regulated by each municipal Fiscal Ordinance, which is why specific figures vary by municipality and are updated periodically in the BOPB (the Barcelona provincial official gazette). The ICIO (Tax on Constructions, Installations and Works) typically represents between 2% and 4% of the construction budget. The planning fee itself varies by municipality. Bonds for waste management are calculated proportionally to the estimated volume. The environmental licence applies only in some specific cases, not in most single-family residential projects.

Each Town Hall publishes its current Fiscal Ordinance in the BOPB. PAPIK verifies the applicable amount for each specific project during the financial planning phase.

---

## H2 · 4. Foundations and earthworks

Foundations are the other cost that is often underestimated in early approximations. They depend mainly on the type of ground (rock, clay, sandy, high water table, each with different implications), the slope (a flat plot is substantially cheaper than one with a steep slope), the mandatory geotechnical study that defines the appropriate foundation solution, and any retaining walls if the plot requires significant earthworks or excavation.

In projects in areas such as Bellaterra (sloping plots) or Matadepera (natural setting with variable topography), this component can be a significant percentage of the total budget and must be planned with particular care to avoid deviations during construction.

---

## H2 · 5. Structure and envelope

This is where much of the final cost and energy performance of the home is decided. The structure can be light timber framing, CLT (cross-laminated timber), concrete or mixed systems, each with implications for cost, thermal behaviour and construction schedule. Thermal insulation combines materials such as blown cellulose, high-density mineral wool, cork, EPS or XPS, with different choices according to local climate and envelope type. The exterior finish can be SATE (external thermal insulation composite system), ventilated facade with timber or composite, natural cork or stone, depending on the project and applicable ordinance. Common roofs are pitched with ceramic tile, flat with waterproofing, or metal sheet, with the choice conditioned by municipal ordinance in towns such as Matadepera.

Windows are a central element in Passivhaus. We work with timber, aluminium, PVC or mixed systems, always with triple glazing and inert gas chambers to meet the standard.

According to the Catalan Construction Technology Institute (ITeC), the indicative ranges for new residential construction in 2025-2026 in Catalonia sit between €1,500 and €2,500/m² depending on quality and system. A Passivhaus build with a lightweight system usually sits in the mid-high range of this band, with payback through energy savings within seven to ten years.

This range is indicative of the public sector. The specific cost of each project depends on case-specific factors and is only defined with a detailed technical study of the plot and the brief.

---

## H2 · 6. Building services and systems

A Passivhaus home incorporates specific systems: heat-recovery ventilation (typically Zehnder or equivalent), aerothermal climate control with underfloor heating or fan coils, energy generation with photovoltaic panels and optionally a self-consumption battery, domestic hot water integrated with the aerothermal system, and optional home automation integrating all systems (Loxone, KNX or equivalent).

Standard security systems are largely already covered by the Passivhaus construction itself, which is why they do not usually represent a relevant additional budget item. The full set of services and systems can add between 15% and 25% to the base construction budget, depending on the level of integration desired by the client.

---

## H2 · 7. Interior finishes

Interior finishes are the most customisable component and the one with the widest spread between economical and premium options. The kitchen can be basic, mid-high or premium range. Bathrooms offer basic, intermediate or premium fixtures. Floors are resolved with ceramic, porcelain stoneware, parquet or microcement. Interior carpentry can be standard, hardwood or lacquered. Paints are chosen without volatile organic compounds (VOCs), with mineral or conventional finishes depending on preference.

At PAPIK we offer standardised configurations for each range, although the spread is wide. Variation between basic and premium finishes can add between 10% and 25% to the final project budget.

---

## H2 · 8. External factors that also count

Beyond the seven main components, there are external factors to incorporate into the overall cost calculation: applicable VAT (10% for housing in Spain, to be confirmed with tax advice for each specific case), professional fees (architect, technical architect, building services engineers), the cost of managing the owning company if construction is carried out through a corporate vehicle, and the financial cost of the applicable mortgage throughout the loan term.

---

## H2 · 9. Why Passivhaus makes economic sense over a 30-year horizon

A Passivhaus build typically involves an additional cost of 5% to 15% over the same construction with basic CTE (Spanish Technical Building Code) standards, according to public sector sources such as IDAE and the PEP Platform.

This additional cost is recovered through several channels that operate over the asset's useful life. Operational energy savings come first: a Passivhaus home consumes between 70% and 90% less than an equivalent conventional home over its entire useful life. The useful life is longer, with structure and envelope offering verifiable durability beyond one hundred years. Maintenance is reduced, since systems operate with less thermal wear and, consequently, with less need for repairs. Market revaluation is higher, with studies from Sociedad de Tasación (a leading Spanish appraisal firm) and Idealista Data (the data unit of the largest Spanish real estate portal) indicating that a Class A energy rating home revalues between 15% and 25% more than an equivalent Class E or F home.

The financial break-even point, defined as the moment at which accumulated savings offset the initial additional cost, usually sits between seven and twelve years. From that point on, every additional year of useful life is net benefit for the owner.

## H2 · 11. Frequently asked questions

### What is the cost difference between a standard home and a Passivhaus?

Typically an additional cost of 5% to 15% in construction, recoverable in seven to twelve years through energy savings. After amortisation, every year is net benefit for the owner.

### Which construction system is the most cost-effective?

It depends on the specific case. Light timber framing tends to be the most economical for residential Passivhaus. CLT has a higher initial cost but superior durability and performance. Traditional construction with energy upgrades has intermediate cost but lower final efficiency, which is why it is less common in projects seeking full Passivhaus certification.

### Can a Passivhaus home be built on a tight budget?

Yes. The Passivhaus Standard versions are accessible to most families with standard mortgage financing capacity. The Passivhaus Plus and Premium versions are premium typologies with additional cost. Each client chooses the level that makes sense for their specific case.

### What exactly does the cost given by the configurator include?

The construction cost estimate based on the parameters entered. It does not include plot, permits, fees, VAT, or professional fees, which are communicated separately in the PDF that is generated.

### How can I finance my Passivhaus home?

Through a conventional mortgage or, in some cases, a green mortgage (a loan with preferential terms for high energy-efficiency homes) with specific bonuses for high-efficiency homes. PAPIK has an agreement with Banc Sabadell that facilitates this: see [our article on the green mortgage](/en/article-sabadell-press-release).
