## INTRO

Building inside the Montseny Natural Park, a protected mountain massif north of Barcelona in Catalonia, is not the same as building anywhere else. Planning restrictions are strict, site logistics are limited, the terrain has steep slopes, and the climate swings between cold winters with frequent frost and hot, humid summers. Every project decision carries implications that simply do not exist on a conventional urban plot.

K-Alzina is a single-family home of 180 m² we built on the slopes of the Montseny. The brief was clear: a house consistent with its natural surroundings, with the lowest possible environmental impact, certifiable to the Passivhaus (passive house) standard for comfort and energy efficiency. This article explains how we did it, what we learned and what results have been measured.

---

## H2 · The challenge: permits, access and climate

The first obstacle was administrative. Building inside the perimeter of the Natural Park requires specific environmental reports, height and volumetric limits, colour restrictions for exterior finishes, and a construction-waste management plan approved by the park authority. The permit process was substantially longer than for a comparable urban project.

Site access was the second challenge. The entry track, narrow and with tight bends, limited the size of delivery vehicles. That constraint shaped the entire programme: prefabricated framing elements had to fit medium-sized trucks, and material deliveries had to be staged in phases so that natural ground outside the authorised work zone was never used as storage.

The Montseny climate completed the picture. Winter temperatures regularly drop below 0 °C, with peaks of minus five or minus seven during cold spells. In summer, the combination of heat and humidity demands a building envelope that handles water vapour properly. The design had to perform at both ends of the spectrum at the same time.

---

## H2 · Design: orientation and form in the service of climate

The architectural project, by Arquitir studio, starts from an L-shaped plan that wraps the terrain and creates a south-facing courtyard. The layout responds to specific thermal criteria. It maximises passive solar gain in winter (low sun reaches deep into the main rooms), while the wings of the building shield the courtyard from prevailing northerly winds.

Openings were calculated with PHPP (Passive House Planning Package) to balance solar gains against the risk of summer overheating. On the south facade, large-format windows include movable external solar shading (sliding larch shutters) that block the summer sun without giving up the views. On the east and west facades the openings are more restrained, to limit unwanted gains during summer afternoons.

> On the Montseny the challenge is not only keeping the house warm in winter. The combination of heat and humidity in summer demands a design that breathes without losing airtightness. Every construction detail is designed for both extremes.

---

## H2 · Structure and materials: FSC-certified timber throughout

The skeleton of K-Alzina is built entirely from FSC-certified timber, using a lightweight fir frame that combines structural performance with thermal efficiency. FSC certification was a non-negotiable requirement, consistent with a site inside a natural park: every piece of timber comes from responsibly managed forests, with full chain-of-custody traceability.

The thermal envelope combines expanded cork insulation on the outside (SATE, an external insulation finishing system, 8 cm) with blown cellulose inside the framing cavities (16 cm). The combination delivers demanding U-values: 0.15 W/m²K on external walls, 0.12 W/m²K on the roof, 0.18 W/m²K on the ground slab and an average of 0.85 W/m²K on the timber-aluminium triple-glazed windows.

The ventilated facade pairs larch timber slats in daytime areas with white SATE in night-time areas, a chromatic contrast accepted within the park's guidelines. Inside, painted plasterboard surfaces sit alongside exposed laminated-timber walls, a choice that adds warmth and reinforces the link with the project's lead material.

## H2 · Energy performance: the numbers

K-Alzina's energy performance has been validated by measurement and certification. The blower-door test returned 0.35 ACH at 50 Pa, well below the Passivhaus limit of 0.6 ACH. That figure reflects careful execution of the airtightness layer, with particular attention to service penetrations and panel-to-panel joints.

The heating demand calculated with PHPP sits at 14 kWh/m²·a, and the cooling demand at 11 kWh/m²·a. Both figures meet the Passivhaus limit of 15 kWh/m²·a for each end use. The mechanical ventilation with heat recovery, delivered with a Zehnder ComfoAir Q350 unit, recovers more than 90 % of the energy from the exhaust air, a decisive feature during the cold months in the Montseny.

The house uses no gas. Heating, cooling and domestic hot water are all handled by an air-source heat pump. The photovoltaic array, paired with battery storage, covers a significant share of annual electricity consumption.

---

## H2 · Carbon balance: what the figures say

What makes K-Alzina distinctive is its favourable carbon balance. According to the life-cycle assessment (LCA) and compared with an equivalent conventional concrete-and-masonry build, the project saved 23.90 tonnes of CO₂ in construction emissions. In addition, the structural timber has absorbed and stores 41.82 tonnes of atmospheric CO₂, in line with the methodology of Environmental Product Declarations (EPD) for the timber sector.

The net balance is therefore clearly favourable. Instead of emitting tens of tonnes of carbon as a conventional build would, K-Alzina behaves as a carbon sink: the building stores more CO₂ than the construction process generated.

---

## H2 · Connecting inside and outside

A guiding principle of the project is the gradual dissolution of the boundary between the inhabited space and the landscape. The large openings on the south facade frame views toward the Montseny and flood the main rooms with daylight. The covered porch, sitting at the intersection of the two wings of the L, works as an outdoor living room for much of the year.

The treatment of the immediate ground reinforces that integration. Gardens of native, low-water plants replace conventional lawn, and a rainwater harvesting system feeds drip irrigation. The medium-term aim is for the garden to merge into the surrounding forest.

---

## H2 · What sets K-Alzina apart from other PAPIK projects

Every PAPIK project has its specifics, but K-Alzina stands out on several counts. Compared with K-Llavaneres (198 m², the studio's first Passivhaus) or K-Codines (212.75 m²), K-Alzina was the first project subject to the constraints of a natural park, with all that entails for logistics, externally visible materials and waste handling.

It was also the project where we consolidated our passive solar shading system with sliding shutters, a solution carried forward into later builds such as K-Valld'Or (280 m²). And it confirmed that the construction system performs under demanding climatic conditions: if lightweight framing and a Passivhaus envelope work on the Montseny, they work anywhere in Catalonia.

> K-Alzina proved that building inside a protected area does not mean giving anything up. It means doing it better, with more attention to detail and more respect for the place.

## CLOSING

For the owners, the result is a home where indoor temperature stays stable year-round without constant intervention, where the air is filtered and continuously renewed by the MVHR system, and where energy bills are a fraction of what they would pay in a conventional house of the same size. K-Alzina shows that building responsibly on the Montseny is not utopian: it is a question of knowing how to do it.
