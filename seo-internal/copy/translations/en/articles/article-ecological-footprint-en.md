The ecological footprint of building and living in your home

## INTRO

A home has two ecological footprints that are often confused: that of its construction and that of its operation over the years. Traditionally, sustainable architecture has focused on reducing operational consumption, that is, the second one. But in a Passivhaus home, operational consumption is reduced so much that the impact of construction can exceed that of operation across the building's service life.

This article analyses how each footprint is measured under the European standard EN 15978, why the global balance of a Passivhaus home can be drastically better than that of a conventional construction, and how to make decisions with data instead of intuitions.

---

## H2 · 1. What the ecological footprint of a home actually measures

The standard EN 15978 (Sustainability of construction works · Assessment of environmental performance of buildings · Calculation method) defines how a building's environmental impact across its complete life cycle must be analysed. It establishes five analysis stages.

Stages A1 to A3 cover the product: raw material extraction, transport and material manufacturing. Stages A4 and A5 cover construction proper: transport to site and the construction process. Stages B1 to B7 cover use: operation, maintenance, repair, replacement, operational energy and water. Stages C1 to C4 cover end of life: demolition, transport, waste treatment and final disposal. Stage D captures benefits beyond the system boundary (reuse, recycling).

A serious assessment of a home's ecological footprint must include all five stages. When someone speaks of a "sustainable house" without citing EN 15978 or LCA (Life Cycle Assessment) or EPD (Environmental Product Declaration), the conversation is at the marketing level and not at the engineering level.

---

## H2 · 2. A1-A3 footprint: the materials that enter the house

The initial stages of the life cycle are the ones that bring the most differentiation between projects. Here you see whether a house has been built with materials of renewable origin, low embodied energy and verifiable traceability, or with industrial materials of high footprint.

The indicative comparison based on technical literature in the sector places concrete around 0.15 kg CO₂/kg, structural steel between 1.8 and 2.0 kg CO₂/kg, mineral wool around 0.5 kg CO₂/kg, blown cellulose (recycled paper) between 0.1 and 0.2 kg CO₂/kg, FSC laminated timber between 0.3 and 0.5 kg CO₂/kg with prior CO₂ capture from the tree, and natural cork with net positive capture during the growth of the cork oak.

A house with CLT timber structure, cellulose insulation and cork cladding can have stored carbon, not just "low emissions". In other words, the construction has extracted CO₂ from the atmosphere and keeps it captured within the timber and the cork while the building exists. This nuance turns the construction decision into an active climate mitigation tool, not only a passive one.

---

## H2 · 3. B6 footprint: operational energy across 50 years

Stage B6 measures operational energy consumption during the building's service life. This is where the Passivhaus standard makes the most spectacular difference.

A conventional house built to the basic CTE (Spanish Technical Building Code) consumes between 70 and 150 kWh/m²·a for climate control, depending on climate and typology. A Passivhaus home is designed to consume less than 15 kWh/m²·a for the same purpose, the certification threshold of the Passivhaus Institut in Darmstadt.

The accumulated difference over fifty years, for an average home of two hundred square metres, is substantial. A conventional house consumes the equivalent of 1,000,000 kWh during this period (200 m² × 100 kWh/m²·a × 50 years). A Passivhaus home consumes the equivalent of 150,000 kWh for the same purpose (200 m² × 15 kWh/m²·a × 50 years). The difference, 850,000 kWh saved, is equivalent to approximately 170 to 220 tonnes of CO₂ not emitted, depending on the applicable average electricity mix.

---

## H2 · 4. The global balance of a Passivhaus home

When all life cycle stages are added under EN 15978, the pattern that appears is consistent. The construction footprint (stages A1 to A5) is usually higher in a Passivhaus than in a conventional one, because it incorporates more insulation, more technical windows and added mechanical systems. The operational footprint (stage B6) is drastically lower in Passivhaus for the reasons described in the previous section. End of life (stages C and D) is similar between typologies, depending on the specific materials used.

The break-even point, defined as the moment from which the footprint of building Passivhaus is offset by the accumulated operational saving, usually sits between five and ten years from project handover. From that moment on, each additional year of the building's service life adds net benefit in environmental terms.

For a home designed to last one hundred years, the global environmental balance of a Passivhaus construction is clearly positive compared with an equivalent conventional construction, with an accumulated difference measured in hundreds of tonnes of CO₂ across the service life.

## H2 · 5. Documented cases: what we have actually measured

PAPIK has applied this analysis framework to several of its own projects. The [Passive House in La Floresta](/en/projecte-la-floresta) (Sant Cugat) was the first project that combined CLT with cellulose, with an estimated break-even point between six and eight years compared with an equivalent basic CTE construction. [K-Iturbi](/en/projecte-k-iturbi) (Bellaterra), Passivhaus certified with Eskimohaus®, has an estimated accumulated reduction greater than 170 tonnes of CO₂ over fifty years compared with a conventional equivalent. [K-Llavaneres](/en/projecte-k-llavaneres) (Maresme), the first Passivhaus Plus in Catalonia, combines photovoltaic production and battery with the Passivhaus standard to achieve a practically neutral operational energy balance.

Each project has specific parameters, which is why the concrete figures are indicative based on PHPP simulation (Passive House Planning Package) and not results directly measured across fifty years of operation.

## H2 · 6. What you can do: decisions with real impact

If you are weighing up building or retrofitting a home and you want the decision to have real environmental impact and not only aesthetic impact, there are four criteria that guide a serious assessment.

Requesting an LCA on the project is the first. The tools exist (EnergyPlus, OneClick LCA, PHPP, WUFI), and if the builder cannot provide one, the conversation is being held at the marketing level and not the engineering level. Verifying the EPDs of the main materials (structure, insulation, claddings) is the second. A serious manufacturer provides them; one that does not have them probably does not meet the four basic criteria. Not confusing "ecological" with "sustainable" is the third: materials of natural origin but with high embodied energy, such as rock wool imported from the other side of Europe, can be a worse option than recycled local industrial materials. Thinking in fifty years, not in five, is the fourth: a construction decision has impact across the entire service life of the building, and the difference between "a little less efficient" and "much more efficient" can be hundreds of tonnes of accumulated CO₂.

## H2 · 7. Frequently asked questions

### What is an EPD?

Environmental Product Declaration. Standardised technical document under ISO 14025 that certifies the environmental impact of a material across its life cycle. More and more manufacturers offer it, and its absence is a warning sign.

### Is the LCA calculation expensive?

It is variable. For a single-family residential project, a basic LCA assessment can be incorporated into the project at relatively low cost if it is integrated from the initial design. Detailed LCA assessment with third-party verifiers has a higher cost and is only relevant in projects with specific certifications such as LEED or BREEAM.

### Does timber sequester carbon indefinitely?

While the timber forms part of a building and is not burned or biodegraded, the captured carbon remains sequestered. After the building's service life, the timber can be recycled or, if it goes to a waste manager correctly, it can maintain the temporary sequestration in other applications.

### How can I verify a builder's environmental claims?

By requesting formal certifications (Passivhaus PHI, FSC, PEFC, LEED, BREEAM), EPDs of the main materials, PHPP simulation of thermal behaviour, and LCA calculation under EN 15978. If all of this is missing, the claims are advertising.
