The five Passivhaus principles

## INTRO

The Passivhaus standard, developed by the Passivhaus Institut (PHI) in Darmstadt, defines energy-performance criteria strict enough that a home needs only a minimal amount of energy to maintain interior comfort year-round. Annual heating and cooling demand must each stay below 15 kWh/m²a, which in practice translates into consumption reductions of between 75% and 90% compared with conventional construction in the climate of Catalonia.

The standard does not prescribe specific materials or solutions. It defines outcomes, leaving each project free to deliver them with the combination best suited to its context. What it does prescribe are five fundamental principles that no Passivhaus project can omit: continuous thermal insulation, envelope airtightness, thermal-bridge elimination, mechanical ventilation with heat recovery, and high-performance windows. The five operate as an integrated system. This article describes them one by one, as you will find them applied within PAPIK's Eskimohaus® construction system.

---

## H2 · 1. Continuous thermal insulation

Insulation is the first line of defence against energy losses. In a Passivhaus home, the full envelope (walls, roof, and ground slab) carries insulation thicknesses well above those of construction limited to meeting the Spanish Technical Building Code (CTE). While the CTE requires thermal transmittances (U-values) of between 0.35 and 0.56 W/m²K for walls depending on climate zone, a Passivhaus typically works with values between 0.12 and 0.15 W/m²K in walls and below 0.12 W/m²K in roofs.

The key concept is continuity. Insulation must wrap the structure without interruption, because every interruption becomes a localised loss point that degrades overall performance. In PAPIK's Eskimohaus® construction system, the envelope integrates between 20 and 30 centimetres of total insulation, distributed between the structural cavity of the lightweight timber frame and a continuous external layer. The usual materials are blown cellulose, wood fibre, and high-density mineral wool, selected for each project according to geometry, orientation, and hygrothermal demands. For renovation work, where SATE (external thermal insulation composite system) provides external continuity without losing interior surface area, the logic is the same: every centimetre is calculated to maximise system efficiency, not to satisfy a regulatory minimum.

---

## H2 · 2. Envelope airtightness

Airtightness is the envelope's ability to block uncontrolled air infiltration. It does not mean the house is a sealed box. It means that air enters and leaves exclusively through the controlled mechanical ventilation system, not through gaps, poorly sealed joints, or badly resolved service penetrations. Conventional construction can show involuntary air-change rates (n50) between 8 and 15 h⁻¹, while the Passivhaus standard requires values below 0.6 h⁻¹.

Verification is performed using the blower-door test, which depressurises the dwelling to 50 Pa and measures the air leakage. If the result fails the threshold, the project is not certified. In PAPIK's projects, results typically land between 0.3 and 0.5 h⁻¹, well below the limit, thanks to a sealing discipline applied from the very first construction detail. Continuous airtight membranes between structure and finish, specific tapes for joints and service penetrations, airtight backboxes for sockets on external walls, and careful resolution of every wall-to-roof junction. Workshop fabrication of frame elements allows the membranes to be integrated under controlled conditions, which improves seal quality compared with on-site execution exposed to weather.

---

## H2 · 3. Thermal-bridge elimination

A thermal bridge is any point in the envelope where thermal resistance is lower than the rest. The consequences are localised heat losses, drops in interior surface temperature, and frequently condensation with mould risk. In conventional construction, the most common thermal bridges appear at slab edges, window lintels, corners, and wall-to-roof junctions.

In Passivhaus, these critical points are eliminated during the design phase, not solved on the fly during construction. Each construction detail is modelled with two-dimensional thermal-simulation software (THERM or equivalent) to verify that the linear thermal-transmittance coefficient (Ψ) stays below 0.01 W/mK. In PAPIK's lightweight timber-frame system, the geometric continuity of the external insulation is relatively straightforward to achieve, because the insulation wraps the entire structure without interruption and junctions are resolved with specific low-conductivity components. The result is a thermally homogeneous envelope, free of cold spots, where interior surface temperature stays uniform across every wall. That uniformity removes the radiant cold sensation many conventional houses suffer near external walls, and it is one of the factors that contributes most to perceived comfort.

---

## H2 · 4. Mechanical ventilation with heat recovery

An airtight dwelling needs controlled mechanical ventilation. Without it, indoor air quality deteriorates and humidity problems appear. This principle closes the loop of the Passivhaus system: a mechanical ventilation unit with heat recovery extracts stale air from kitchen, bathrooms, and humid rooms and delivers filtered fresh air to living rooms and bedrooms, with a heat exchanger that transfers thermal energy from the outgoing air to the incoming air without mixing the streams.

Recovery efficiency in current units sits between 85% and 92%, with modest electrical consumption under normal operation. In PAPIK's projects we typically use Zehnder, the European reference for residential ventilation, sized for each project according to dwelling geometry and projected occupancy. The system incorporates F7 filters that retain pollen, fine particles, and pollutants, which is why indoor air quality in a Passivhaus is noticeably better than in any home relying on natural window ventilation. The user gets continuously refreshed air at ambient temperature, with no draughts or sudden changes, and stable relative humidity throughout the year. Many of our clients with allergies or asthma report a clear improvement in symptoms within the first few months of use.

## H2 · 5. High-performance windows

Windows are the thermal weak point of any envelope. In conventional construction they may show U-values between 2.5 and 3.5 W/m²K, accounting for up to 25% of heat losses. The Passivhaus standard requires a Uw (whole-window transmittance, frame plus glazing) below 0.85 W/m²K, a value that PAPIK's projects typically place between 0.70 and 0.80 W/m²K.

The technology that makes this possible combines several integrated elements: triple glazing with argon or krypton-filled cavities between panes, low-emissivity (low-e) coatings on the glass to reduce thermal radiation, low-conductivity warm-edge spacers (in place of traditional metal ones), and insulated frames in timber, timber-aluminium, or premium-grade PVC with multiple chambers. Installation is as critical as the window itself: the leaf is positioned inside the wall's insulation plane to avoid thermal bridges around the frame perimeter, and airtight sealing is integrated with the rest of the envelope. The solar factor (g-value) is tuned per opening orientation: higher on north-facing facades to maximise daylight, lower on south and west facades to control summer solar gain, a calibration that carries more weight in the climate of Catalonia than in central-European latitudes.

## H2 · 6. Why the five principles work as a system

The five principles do not work in isolation. What allows consumption reductions of 75% to 90% is their integration into a coherent system, where each principle reinforces the others. A house with excellent insulation but insufficient airtightness loses thermal energy through uncontrolled infiltration, cancelling much of the insulation investment. A well-insulated envelope with unresolved thermal bridges generates interior condensation despite correct theoretical performance. High airtightness without mechanical ventilation degrades indoor air quality and triggers humidity problems. Conventional windows in a high-performance envelope shift the problem rather than solving it.

The PHI standard requires all five because it is the combination that produces the outcome, not any single one in isolation. Formal certification by the Darmstadt institute verifies that the project modelled with PHPP (Passive House Planning Package) meets the energy-demand thresholds and that on-site execution confirms it through the final blower-door test. For deep retrofit, the equivalent standard is EnerPHit, with thresholds adjusted to the geometric constraints inherent in working on an existing building.

---

## H2 · 7. The Mediterranean context: solar protection as a sixth axis

The Passivhaus standard was born in central Europe, where winter cold is the main challenge. In Mediterranean climates, hot summers are often the limiting factor, which is why in PAPIK's projects solar protection is treated with the same rigour as any of the five principles. Calculated overhangs that block the high summer sun while letting the low winter sun through, adjustable external shutters, glazing with orientation-tuned solar factor, and, where viable, deciduous vegetation on south and west facades. Shading studies are carried out from the preliminary-design stage with real local climate data, and the 8,760-hour annual hourly simulation validates summer performance before the project reaches the construction site.

## H2 · 8. What it means for the end client

A house that correctly integrates the five principles maintains interior temperature within a narrow range of 19 to 25 degrees Celsius year-round, with minimal HVAC intervention. It refreshes the air continuously without draughts or energy losses. The annual energy bill drops between 75% and 90% compared with equivalent conventional construction. Market revaluation, according to property-sector studies, runs between 15% and 25% above an equivalent class E or F dwelling.

The cost premium over conventional construction of similar quality typically sits between 10% and 15%, and is recovered over the building's service life through accumulated energy savings. For a single-family residential project in Maresme or Vallès, the question is not whether a Passivhaus makes economic sense. It is whether you want to live in a home that delivers this level of comfort and predictability, because performance is documented to the millimetre from the design stage.

## H2 · 9. Frequently asked questions

### Is formal certification required to obtain these benefits?

No. The five principles deliver thermal and comfort value regardless of formal certification. Certification by the Darmstadt institute is an additional decision that adds processing cost and third-party validation. Many PAPIK clients build to the standard without filing for the official certificate.

### Can it be applied to a renovation?

Yes. The renovation equivalent is EnerPHit, with thresholds adapted to the geometric constraints of an existing building. SATE (external insulation composite system) makes it possible to achieve continuous external insulation without reducing interior surface area, and the five principles apply with the same logic.

### What real difference does the blower-door test make?

It is the difference between a house that meets Passivhaus on paper and a house that meets it in reality. Without blower-door verification there is no verified airtightness, and without verified airtightness much of the insulation investment degrades operationally.

### Which MVHR (mechanical ventilation with heat recovery) unit do you recommend?

At PAPIK we typically use Zehnder for its combination of performance, durability, and European technical support. Sizing is done per project, not by catalogue.
